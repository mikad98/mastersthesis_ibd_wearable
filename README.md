# Master's Thesis - Abstract

Inflammatory bowel diseases (IBD), including Crohnâ€™s disease and ulcerative colitis, are chronic conditions marked by alternating periods of intestinal inflammation and remission. Current monitoring strategies rely largely on intermittent, often invasive assessments and patient-reported symptoms, limiting the timely detection of disease activity and contributing to predominantly reactive models of care. This thesis investigates the potential of multimodal data, combining wearable-derived physiological and sleep metrics with patient-reported outcomes, to enable continuous and personalized monitoring of disease activity and outcomes in IBD. Using a real-world dataset provided by Coreway UG, a digital-health company developing a patient-companion platform that integrates wearable sensor data with daily symptom tracking to support proactive IBD management, this work systematically evaluates the robustness and reproducibility of two landmark studies by \cite{hirten2025, hirten2025sleep}. Linear mixed-effects modeling is employed to examine associations between wearable-derived features and flare periods derived from patient-reported outcomes. In addition to replication analyses, supplementary investigations explore the relationship between wearable-derived metrics and subjective sleep quality, assess anomaly-detection approaches for longitudinal time-series data, and introduce an interactive visualization tool to support future exploratory analyses. The findings largely corroborate prior evidence that wearable-derived physiological and sleep metrics capture meaningful changes surrounding inflammatory flares, while also highlighting challenges related to data heterogeneity and real-world data quality. Overall, this thesis contributes to the growing evidence base supporting wearable technologies as a complementing component of proactive, predictive, and patient-centered digital health solutions in IBD care.
